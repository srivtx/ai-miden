# What Is Input and Output?

Welcome! If you are brand new to AI, this is the perfect place to start. We are going to learn two of the most important ideas in all of computing. You already know basic algebra, so you have everything you need.

---

### 1. Definition (very simple)

An **input** is the information you give to a function.

An **output** is what the function gives back to you after processing the input.

Think of a function as a little machine. You hand it some numbers (or information), it does its job, and it hands you a new number (or result) back.

---

### 2. Why it exists

We separate input from output because it keeps things organized.

If we do not separate them, everything is mixed up and confusing. Imagine trying to bake a cake but you could not tell the difference between the raw eggs (input) and the finished cake (output). That would be chaos!

By clearly labeling what we *know* (input) and what we *want to find out* (output), we can build systems that work step by step.

---

### 3. Real-life analogy

Picture a **vending machine**.

- You walk up and press **button B3**.
- The machine whirs and drops a **bag of chips** into the tray.

In this example:
- **Input** = pressing button B3 (the action you give to the machine)
- **Output** = the bag of chips (the result the machine gives back)

The button press is **not** the chips. They are completely separate things. One is what you *give*, and one is what you *receive*.

---

### 4. Tiny numeric example

Here is a simple math example. Do not worry, there is nothing fancy here.

**Example 1:**
- **Input:** 4 and 5
- **Function:** multiply them together
- **Output:** 20

The inputs are the two numbers you start with. The function is the instruction "multiply." The output is the new number you get after the function does its work.

**Example 2:**
- **Input:** 10
- **Function:** add 3
- **Output:** 13

Again, 10 is what you hand in, "add 3" is the function's job, and 13 is what you get back.

---

### 5. Common confusion

A lot of beginners think the output is "the answer to everything." It is not.

The output is **only** the result of applying that specific function to that specific input. If you change the input, you get a different output.

For example, if your function is "add 3" and your input is 5, the output is 8. But if your input is 100, the output is 103. The function stayed the same, but the input changed, so the output changed too.

Also, remember: **input and output are not the same thing.** One goes in, the other comes out.

---

### 6. Where it is used in our code

In the code we will write together, we will create functions that take **input data** and produce **output predictions**.

For example:
- **Inputs:** house size, number of bedrooms
- **Function:** our AI model
- **Output:** predicted house price

We will feed **inputs** into our AI system and receive **outputs** (predictions). Every time you see the word "input," think "what we know." Every time you see "output," think "what the AI thinks the answer is."
