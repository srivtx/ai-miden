# What Is Alzheimer's Disease?

**The Problem:** Your grandmother is 78. She forgot your name. She forgot her own name. She doesn't recognize her own house. She is becoming someone else. What is happening in her brain? At the molecular level, two proteins are misfolding and accumulating. At the cellular level, neurons — especially in the hippocampus — are dying. At the cognitive level, the most human of all human abilities — memory — is failing. This is Alzheimer's disease.

**Definition:** *Alzheimer's disease* (AD) is a progressive neurodegenerative disorder characterized by the accumulation of two misfolded proteins — *amyloid-β* (Aβ) plaques outside neurons and *tau* tangles inside neurons — leading to widespread neuronal death, particularly in the hippocampus and cortex. The first symptom is typically memory loss; the last is death.

**How It Works (Step-by-Step):**

1. **The macroscopic story.** AD begins in the *entorhinal cortex* and *hippocampus* — the brain's memory system. (See `03_architecture/what_is_hippocampus.md`.) The first symptom is *anterograde amnesia* — inability to form new memories. The disease then spreads to the *cortex* — affecting language, reasoning, personality. Late-stage AD patients cannot recognize family, speak, or feed themselves. Death typically follows 3-12 years after diagnosis.
2. **The microscopic story — amyloid-β plaques.** Aβ is a small peptide (~40-42 amino acids) produced by *cleavage* of the *amyloid precursor protein* (APP), a normal membrane protein. In AD, Aβ *misfolds* into a β-sheet conformation, aggregates into *oligomers* (small toxic clumps), and accumulates as *plaques* (large extracellular deposits). The oligomers are *toxic* to neurons — they disrupt membranes, increase Ca²⁺, and trigger inflammation.
3. **The microscopic story — tau tangles.** *Tau* is a *microtubule-associated protein* — it stabilizes the cytoskeleton of neurons. In AD, tau becomes *hyperphosphorylated* (too many phosphate groups), detaches from microtubules, and forms *neurofibrillary tangles* inside neurons. Tangles disrupt intracellular transport, leading to cell death.
4. **The cascade hypothesis.** The dominant theory: Aβ accumulation comes *first* (decades before symptoms), then triggers tau pathology, then leads to neuronal death, then to symptoms. This is the *amyloid cascade hypothesis* (Hardy & Higgins, 1992). It is supported by genetics (Down syndrome, where APP is triplicated, leads to early AD) but is *controversial* — anti-amyloid drugs have repeatedly failed in clinical trials, suggesting Aβ may be necessary but not sufficient.
5. **The molecular triggers — what causes misfolding?** Genetic risk factors:
   - **APOE ε4 allele:** the strongest genetic risk factor. APOE is involved in lipid transport. ε4 carriers have ~3× (heterozygous) to ~15× (homozygous) increased risk.
   - **APP, PSEN1, PSEN2 mutations:** rare, autosomal dominant, cause *early-onset familial AD* (~1-2% of cases). These mutations increase Aβ production.
   - **TREM2 variants:** microglial dysfunction, suggesting immune system involvement.
   - **Many other risk variants:** identified by GWAS. Each has small effect.
6. **The cellular consequences — why does Aβ cause damage?**
   - **Synaptic dysfunction:** Aβ oligomers bind to receptors at the synapse, disrupting LTP and impairing synaptic plasticity.
   - **Mitochondrial dysfunction:** Aβ enters mitochondria, impairs oxidative phosphorylation, increases ROS.
   - **Calcium dyshomeostasis:** Aβ forms pores in membranes, allowing Ca²⁺ influx → excitotoxicity.
   - **Neuroinflammation:** microglia are activated, release pro-inflammatory cytokines, chronic neuroinflammation damages neurons.
   - **Tau propagation:** misfolded tau can *spread* between neurons in a *prion-like* way — like prion disease.
7. **The brain's compensation.** The brain has *massive* redundancy. By the time symptoms appear, ~30% of neurons in the entorhinal cortex and hippocampus have died. The disease has been progressing for 15-20 years. The brain was compensating through redundancy — only when the redundancy is exhausted do symptoms appear.
8. **The role of BDNF.** AD patients have *reduced BDNF* in hippocampus and cortex. This impairs synaptic plasticity and *growth* — the brain cannot repair itself. BDNF reduction is both a *cause* (less growth → neurons more vulnerable) and a *consequence* (Aβ → inflammation → BDNF reduction) of AD.
9. **The role of sleep.** Recent evidence: *sleep disturbance* may be both a risk factor and an early symptom. The glymphatic system (see `04_systems/what_is_sleep.md`) clears Aβ during deep sleep. Chronic sleep disruption → Aβ accumulation → AD pathology. This is a *two-way* relationship: AD causes sleep disruption, which worsens AD.
10. **The role of metabolism.** AD brains show *impaired glucose metabolism* (FDG-PET shows this decades before symptoms). The cause may be *mitochondrial dysfunction* (impaired oxidative phosphorylation) and *insulin resistance* in the brain (some call AD "type 3 diabetes"). Ketone bodies (an alternative fuel) can partially rescue metabolism — the basis of the ketogenic diet hypothesis.
11. **Treatment.** Current treatments:
    - **Acetylcholinesterase inhibitors** (donepezil, rivastigmine): boost ACh in the brain, partially compensate for cholinergic neuron loss. Modest benefit.
    - **NMDA receptor antagonist** (memantine): blocks excitotoxicity. Modest benefit.
    - **Anti-amyloid antibodies** (lecanemab, donanemab, 2023+): the first disease-modifying drugs. Clear Aβ from the brain. Slow cognitive decline by ~25-30%. But: expensive, require IV infusion, have side effects (brain swelling, microbleeds), and the effect is small.
    - **Lifestyle:** exercise (BDNF↑), cognitive engagement, social interaction, sleep hygiene, Mediterranean diet. All reduce risk.
12. **The future.** Active research:
    - **Tau-targeting therapies:** antibodies, anti-aggregation drugs. May be more effective than anti-Aβ.
    - **Gene therapy:** APOE2 delivery, CRISPR for familial AD.
    - **Neurogenesis:** stimulating new neuron growth in the hippocampus.
    - **Brain-computer interfaces:** to compensate for lost memory.
    - **Prevention:** treating risk factors (sleep, metabolic, vascular) decades before symptoms.

**Real-life analogy:** AD is like a *slow house fire* in a city. The amyloid is the *smoke* — accumulates for years, unnoticed. The tau is the *flame* — the actual destructive force. The neuron death is the *burning buildings*. The symptoms (memory loss) are the *people fleeing*. By the time you see the flames, the fire has been burning for decades. Prevention means detecting the *smoke* early — via biomarkers (CSF Aβ, PET amyloid imaging, blood tests) — and stopping the fire before it starts.

**Tiny numeric example:** A typical AD patient at diagnosis:
- ~30% of hippocampal neurons have died
- ~10% of cortical neurons have died
- Brain weight has decreased by ~10-15%
- Ventricles (CSF-filled cavities) have enlarged by ~50%
- Aβ plaque count: ~10⁶ per cm³ of cortex
- Tau tangle count: ~10⁵ per cm³ of cortex
- Cognitive decline: ~3 points/year on MMSE (Mini-Mental State Exam)
- Life expectancy after diagnosis: 3-12 years
- Total US economic burden: ~$300 billion/year (2020 estimate)
- Global burden: ~50 million people (2020)

**Common confusion:**

- No. "Alzheimer's is just memory loss." Early AD affects *episodic memory* (recent events). Other cognitive domains (language, visuospatial, executive) are also affected, especially later. Late-stage AD is global cognitive decline.
- No. "Alzheimer's is normal aging." No. *Normal* aging has mild memory slowing but not *dementia*. AD is a disease. *Some* cognitive decline is normal; AD is pathological.
- No. "Aluminum causes Alzheimer's." No. Old hypothesis, disproven. No consistent evidence.
- No. "Aβ is the only cause." Probably no. Aβ is necessary but not sufficient. Tau, neuroinflammation, metabolic dysfunction, vascular factors all contribute. AD is *multifactorial*.
- No. "Antiamyloid drugs cure AD." No. They *slow* decline by ~25%. Modest effect. The amyloid hypothesis may be incomplete.
- No. "If you have amyloid plaques, you have AD." Mostly yes, but ~30% of cognitively normal elderly have significant amyloid burden. They are at risk but may not develop dementia in their lifetime. *Reserve* and *resilience* matter.

**Key properties:**

- **Progressive:** symptoms worsen over years.
- **Multifactorial:** Aβ, tau, inflammation, metabolism, vascular.
- **Preclinical phase:** pathology begins 15-20 years before symptoms.
- **Hippocampal origin:** starts in the memory system.
- **Currently incurable:** treatments slow but do not stop.
- **Massive economic burden:** ~$1 trillion/year globally.
- **Common:** ~10% of people over 65, ~30% over 85.
- **Genetic component:** ~60-80% heritable, but most cases are sporadic.

**Where it appears in technology:**

- **AI for early detection:** machine learning on MRI, PET, blood biomarkers predicts AD years before symptoms. (See `08_measuring_the_brain/`.)
- **AI for drug discovery:** deep learning models predict Aβ aggregation, tau phosphorylation, drug binding.
- **AI as cognitive assistant:** language models (LLMs) are being explored as *memory aids* for early AD patients.
- **Brain-computer interfaces:** Neuralink and similar could, in the future, compensate for memory loss by writing to hippocampal circuits.
- **Preventive medicine:** AI risk models identify high-risk patients for early intervention.
