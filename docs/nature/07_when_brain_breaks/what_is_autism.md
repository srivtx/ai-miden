# What Is Autism?

**The Problem:** A child doesn't make eye contact. Doesn't respond to their name. Lines up toys in a row. Hums a single note. Screams when their routine changes. They grow up. They can't read social cues. They are exhausted by small talk. They have a few intense interests. They are not "sick" in any obvious way. But the world is *hard* for them. Is this a disorder? A difference? A different kind of mind? This is the question of *autism*.

**Definition:** *Autism spectrum disorder* (ASD) is a *neurodevelopmental condition* characterized by differences in *social communication and interaction*, alongside *restricted, repetitive patterns of behavior, interests, or activities* (sometimes called stimming). It is *not* a disease to be cured — it is a *spectrum* of neurodevelopmental variation. The neurodiversity movement frames autism as a difference, not a deficit.

**How It Works (Step-by-Step):**

1. **The clinical picture.** Two core domains (DSM-5):
   - **Social communication and interaction:** difficulty with social-emotional reciprocity, nonverbal communication, developing and maintaining relationships.
   - **Restricted, repetitive behaviors:** repetitive movements, insistence on sameness, restricted/fixated interests, atypical sensory responses.
   - Symptoms must be present in early childhood (though may be masked in adults).
   - Spectrum: from individuals who need 24-hour care to those who are indistinguishable from neurotypical adults in casual interactions.
2. **The "triad of impairments" (historical).** Wing & Gould (1979) described three:
   - **Social interaction:** difficulty with peer relationships, lack of social-emotional reciprocity.
   - **Communication:** delayed or absent speech, atypical nonverbal communication.
   - **Imagination:** restricted, repetitive behaviors; lack of pretend play.
   This is the older framing. Modern view: a *spectrum* with multiple dimensions, not three discrete impairments.
3. **The neurocognitive theories — multiple, complementary.**
   - **Theory of mind (ToM):** difficulty understanding others' mental states. Baron-Cohen's "mindblindness" theory. Autistic people may be slower to develop ToM. (But the "double empathy problem" says the issue is mutual — non-autistic people are also mindblind to autistic people.)
   - **Weak central coherence (Frith):** tendency to focus on local details rather than global context. *Detail-oriented* processing. (This is a difference, not necessarily a deficit — useful in some domains.)
   - **Systemizing (Baron-Cohen):** preference for systems that follow rules (mechanical, mathematical, taxonomic) over empathizing with people. Autistic people may *systemize* better than they *empathize*.
   - **Predictive coding dysregulation:** some theories suggest autism involves *over-precise* prediction errors (high precision on low-level features, low precision on social cues). This produces *intense* perception of details and *hypo-responsiveness* to social signals.
   - **Intense world theory (Markram):** hyper-reactivity in local circuits → enhanced perception, attention, memory, emotion → world is *too intense*. Explains both strengths and difficulties.
   - **Monotropism:** attention is focused on a few interests at a time, deeply. Difficult to switch. This explains *restricted interests* and *cognitive strengths* in specific domains.
4. **The brain differences.**
   - **No single "autism brain."** Autism is *etiologically* heterogeneous.
   - **Early brain overgrowth:** some children show accelerated head/brain growth in the first 1-2 years, then plateau. The cortex is *temporally* out of sync — too much growth, then too much pruning.
   - **Synaptic pruning differences:** autism may involve *reduced* or *altered* synaptic pruning. Genes involved: *NRXN1, NLGN3, NLGN4, SHANK3, FMR1, MECP2, CNTNAP2*. Many are *synaptic adhesion molecules* or *scaffolding proteins*.
   - **Mirror neuron system (controversial):** some studies show reduced mirror neuron activity. But the theory is debated.
   - **Amygdala:** volume and activity differences. May underlie social anxiety and emotional processing differences.
   - **Cerebellum:** purkinje cell loss in some cases.
   - **Connectivity:** *long-range underconnectivity, local overconnectivity* (Just et al., 2004). Long-range connections between distant cortical regions are weaker; local connections within a region are stronger. Explains *detail focus* (local) and *integration difficulty* (long-range).
   - **Minicolumns:** narrower in autism (Casanova et al.). Smaller minicolumns = more local processing, less integration.
5. **The genetic architecture.** Heritability ~60-90% (very high). But:
   - **No single "autism gene."** ~100+ genes implicated. Each has small effect.
   - **Common variants:** polygenic. Hundreds of common variants, each with tiny effect. Polygenic risk score explains ~5% of variance.
   - **Rare de novo variants:** higher *de novo* mutation rate in autism than in unaffected siblings. CNVs (copy number variants): 16p11.2, 22q11.2 (DiGeorge), 15q11.2, others.
   - **Syndromic autism:** ~25% of cases have a known genetic cause (Fragile X, Rett, Tuberous Sclerosis, Angelman, etc.). Single-gene disorders that include autism as a feature.
   - **Idiopathic:** ~75% of cases have no known single cause.
   - **Heritability paradox:** high heritability but no major gene. Suggests *many genes of small effect* + *epigenetic* factors + *prenatal environment*.
6. **The environmental factors.**
   - **Advanced parental age** (especially paternal): more de novo mutations in sperm.
   - **Prenatal exposure to valproate, thalidomide, certain pesticides:** increases risk.
   - **Maternal infection, inflammation, stress, diabetes:** modest risk increases.
   - **Perinatal complications:** prematurity, low birth weight, hypoxia.
   - **Disproven:** vaccines do *not* cause autism (Wakefield fraud). The "refrigerator mother" hypothesis is *discredited* (Bettelheim, 1960s — already known to be wrong for 50 years).
7. **The sex difference.** ~4:1 male-to-female ratio. Possible explanations:
   - *Female protective effect:* females require a higher *genetic load* to be autistic. (This is supported by the *extreme male brain* theory and by studies of CNVs.)
   - *Diagnostic bias:* girls are *better* at masking (camouflaging) — they copy social behavior without understanding it. (The "lost girls" of autism.)
   - *Different presentation:* girls may present with less repetitive behavior, more social mimicry.
8. **The neurodiversity framework.** A paradigm shift, primarily led by autistic people themselves:
   - Autism is a *natural variation* in human neurodevelopment, not a *defect*.
   - Differences in social communication, sensory processing, attention style are *differences* — they come with both challenges and strengths.
   - "Treatment" should focus on *support* (reducing suffering, increasing access) — not *normalization*.
   - The medical model and the neurodiversity model are *complementary*, not opposed. Autism can cause real suffering (anxiety, sensory overload, social isolation). This suffering should be addressed. But the *traits themselves* are not pathological.
9. **The strengths.** Autistic people often have:
   - **Detail-oriented perception:** excellent at noticing patterns, errors, anomalies.
   - **Intense focus:** can work for hours on a single task.
   - **Strong memory** (especially for areas of interest).
   - **Systematic thinking:** can build deep, organized knowledge.
   - **Honesty, directness, low social conformity:** can be valuable in many domains.
   - **Many savant abilities:** rare (~1% of autistic people have savant syndrome), but more common in autism than in the general population. Including calendar calculation, music, art, mathematics, memory.
10. **The challenges.** Autism can cause real difficulties:
    - **Social isolation:** not by choice, but by mismatch with social environment.
    - **Sensory overload:** sounds, lights, textures, smells that don't bother others can be overwhelming.
    - **Anxiety and depression:** comorbidity is very high.
    - **Bullying and victimization:** autistic people are 4× more likely to be victimized.
    - **Employment discrimination:** unemployment rate among autistic adults is ~80%.
    - **Co-occurring conditions:** ~70% have at least one co-occurring condition (anxiety, ADHD, depression, epilepsy, sleep disorders, GI issues, sensory processing disorder).
11. **The diagnostic process.** DSM-5 criteria. Multi-disciplinary assessment: developmental pediatrician, child psychologist, speech-language pathologist, occupational therapist, sometimes psychiatrist. Tools:
    - **ADOS-2** (Autism Diagnostic Observation Schedule): gold standard for observation.
    - **ADI-R** (Autism Diagnostic Interview-Revised): parent interview.
    - **Childhood Autism Rating Scale (CARS).**
    - **SRS-2** (Social Responsiveness Scale): screening.
    - No biological test. Diagnosis is *behavioral*.
12. **Treatment — what works.**
    - **Early intervention:** applied behavior analysis (ABA), speech therapy, occupational therapy, social skills training. Most effective when started before age 3.
    - **Educational support:** individualized education programs (IEPs). Inclusion vs. self-contained classrooms.
    - **Occupational therapy:** sensory integration, motor skills, daily living.
    - **Speech-language therapy:** communication (verbal, AAC — augmentative and alternative communication — including sign language and devices).
    - **Social skills training:** explicit teaching of social rules, perspective-taking.
    - **Cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT):** for anxiety, depression, OCD that often co-occur.
    - **Medication:** for *co-occurring* conditions (anxiety, depression, ADHD, seizures, aggression, sleep). No medication treats core autism.
13. **The controversy of "cures."** The neurodiversity movement has raised important questions:
    - **ABA** (Applied Behavior Analysis) is controversial. Some autistic adults report PTSD from childhood ABA. Modern ABA is more child-led, but historical ABA was compliance-based.
    - **"Cure" approaches** (e.g., chelation, MMS) are harmful, unproven, and rejected by the medical community.
    - **Support vs. normalization:** the neurodiversity movement emphasizes *support* (helping autistic people navigate a non-autistic world) rather than *normalization* (making autistic people act non-autistic). The line is debated.
14. **The future.**
    - **Earlier identification:** genetic risk scores, eye-tracking,语音 analysis. Identify autism at 6-12 months.
    - **Better support:** employment programs, housing, communication aids. Focus on *quality of life*, not "normalization."
    - **AI assistants:** for social coaching, scheduling, sensory management.
    - **Communication technology:** AAC devices, eye-tracking, brain-computer interfaces. Some non-speaking autistic people are *fluent* typers when given the right interface.
    - **Neurodiversity-affirming therapy:** therapy that respects autistic identity while addressing suffering.

**Real-life analogy:** Autism is like being a *mac OS computer in a Windows world*. You are *fully functional* — your hardware is different, your processing is different, your input/output is different. Most of the world's software is built for Windows. You can run some Windows software (with effort, sometimes with errors). You can do *some things* better than Windows (specific processing, parallel computation, consistency). The challenge is not that you are *broken* — it's that the world is built for a different operating system. The solution is *better translation* (more accessible software, better support), not *switching to Windows*.

**Tiny numeric example:** Autism in numbers:
- Global prevalence: ~1 in 100 (~75 million people)
- Sex ratio: ~4:1 male-to-female (closer to 3:1 when less biased diagnosis is used)
- Heritability: ~60-90% (twin studies)
- Genetic contribution: hundreds of genes; polygenic risk score explains ~5% of variance
- Brain volume: ~5-10% larger in young children with autism; normalizes by adolescence
- Synaptic pruning: possibly reduced
- Connectivity: long-range underconnectivity, local overconnectivity
- Comorbidities: ~70% have at least one (anxiety, ADHD, depression, epilepsy, sleep, GI)
- IQ: highly variable. ~30% have intellectual disability; ~30% have above-average IQ
- Employment: ~80% of autistic adults are unemployed or underemployed
- Education: only ~30% complete college
- Cost: lifetime cost ~$2 million per person (US); ~$300 billion/year (US total)
- Diagnosis age: ~3-5 years old (but can be earlier or later)
- "Lost girls": girls are diagnosed 1-2 years later on average

**Common confusion:**

- No. "Autism is caused by vaccines." No. Wakefield (1998) was a *fraud*. Numerous large studies (1.25M+ children) confirm no link. This is settled science.
- No. "Autism is caused by bad parenting." No. The "refrigerator mother" theory is *discredited*. Parenting style affects *course* but not *cause*.
- No. "Autism is one thing." No. Autism is a *spectrum* — from non-speaking individuals with significant support needs to "high-masking" individuals who appear neurotypical in casual interactions.
- No. "All autistic people have savant abilities." No. Savant syndrome is rare (~1% of autistic people) but more common than in the general population.
- No. "Autistic people don't feel emotions." No. They often feel *intensely*. They may have difficulty *expressing* or *regulating* emotions. The alexithymia (difficulty identifying emotions) is common but not universal.
- No. "Autism is a recent phenomenon." No. Autism-like descriptions exist in historical records. The *increase in diagnosis* is largely due to *broader diagnostic criteria* and *greater awareness*, not a true increase in prevalence. (Though a small true increase from environmental factors is possible.)

**Key properties:**

- **Neurodevelopmental:** present from early childhood; brain develops differently.
- **Spectrum:** highly variable in presentation and support needs.
- **Heritable:** ~60-90% heritability, but polygenic + environmental.
- **Multifactorial:** genetic + prenatal + early environment.
- **Not a disease:** a *difference* in neurodevelopment. Causes suffering in some contexts.
- **Co-occurrence:** ~70% have at least one other condition.
- **Strengths + challenges:** the difference includes both. Not a simple deficit.
- **Neurodiversity:** one of many natural variations in human cognition.

**Where it appears in technology:**

- **AI for early detection:** eye-tracking (where children look),语音 analysis (speech patterns), gesture analysis. Detects autism 6-18 months earlier than standard diagnosis.
- **AI for communication:** AAC devices, language models that predict text input. Some non-speaking autistic people are *fluent* when given the right interface.
- **AI for employment matching:** platforms that match autistic workers to suitable jobs (detail-oriented, low-social).
- **AI for therapy:** chatbots delivering CBT, virtual reality for social skills training.
- **AI for emotion recognition:** helping autistic people interpret others' facial expressions (controversial — some autistic adults reject this as "teaching us to mask").
- **Robotic companions:** for social skills practice, especially for children.
- **BCI for non-speaking autism:** enabling communication for those who cannot type or speak.

**Connection to the rest of the curriculum:** Autism is the most controversial disorder because it is *not clearly a disorder*. It is a *kind of mind*. The neurodiversity framework says: the goal is not to *cure* autism, but to *support* autistic people in a world built for non-autistic people. This is the *deepest* question in the brain/mind interface: what is a "normal" brain, and who gets to define it?
