# What Is Schizophrenia?

**The Problem:** A 22-year-old man hears voices commenting on his actions. He believes the TV is sending him messages. He stops going to work, stops showering, speaks in loose, disconnected sentences. His family is terrified. He has schizophrenia. What has gone wrong in his brain? The answer is more complex than for any other major psychiatric disorder — but the *dopamine hypothesis* remains the dominant theory.

**Definition:** *Schizophrenia* is a chronic psychiatric disorder characterized by *positive symptoms* (hallucinations, delusions, disorganized thought), *negative symptoms* (avolition, alogia, anhedonia, blunted affect), and *cognitive symptoms* (working memory, executive function deficits). It typically begins in late adolescence or early adulthood. The lifetime prevalence is ~1%.

**How It Works (Step-by-Step):**

1. **The clinical picture.** Three symptom clusters:
   - **Positive symptoms:** *added* to normal experience — hallucinations (often auditory, "voices"), delusions (persecutory, referential, bizarre), disorganized speech/behavior, movement abnormalities.
   - **Negative symptoms:** *subtracted* from normal experience — avolition (lack of motivation), alogia (poverty of speech), anhedonia (inability to feel pleasure), blunted affect (flat emotional expression), asociality.
   - **Cognitive symptoms:** working memory deficits, attention problems, executive dysfunction. Present in 70%+ of patients. Often the *most disabling* in long-term outcome.
2. **The dopamine hypothesis.** Since the 1970s, the dominant theory:
   - **Positive symptoms** are caused by *excess dopamine* in the *subcortical mesolimbic pathway* (VTA → nucleus accumbens).
   - **Negative symptoms** are caused by *deficient dopamine* in the *prefrontal cortex* (mesocortical pathway).
   - Evidence: all effective antipsychotics are *D2 receptor antagonists*. Amphetamine (which releases dopamine) can cause *psychosis* in non-schizophrenic individuals. PET shows increased dopamine release in striatum in schizophrenia.
3. **The glutamate hypothesis.** A second major hypothesis: *NMDA receptor hypofunction* on cortical interneurons (especially parvalbumin+ GABAergic interneurons) leads to *disinhibition* of pyramidal neurons, *excess glutamate release*, and *excitotoxicity*. Evidence: NMDA antagonists (ketamine, PCP) cause *schizophrenia-like* symptoms in healthy individuals. Genetic risk variants implicate NMDA receptor signaling.
4. **The neurodevelopmental hypothesis.** Schizophrenia is now thought to be a *neurodevelopmental disorder* — caused by abnormal brain development *long before* symptoms appear. Evidence:
   - Prenatal exposure to infection, malnutrition, hypoxia, or maternal stress increases risk.
   - Childhood adversities (urban upbringing, immigration, abuse) increase risk.
   - Minor physical anomalies (e.g., craniofacial) are more common in schizophrenia — suggests early developmental disruption.
   - Premorbid cognitive deficits (subtle IQ decline, motor abnormalities) are present years before psychosis.
   - The *prodromal phase* (months to years) precedes the first psychotic episode.
5. **The two-hit model.** A common framework:
   - **First hit (prenatal):** genetic vulnerability + environmental insult → abnormal brain development. Cortical circuits are subtly mis-wired.
   - **Second hit (adolescence/young adulthood):** cortical pruning (normal in adolescence) is *excessive* — pruning too many synapses. The mis-wired circuit is *exposed*. Symptoms emerge.
6. **The neurochemical cascade.**
   - **Dopamine:** excess in subcortical (positive symptoms), deficient in cortical (negative, cognitive).
   - **Glutamate:** NMDA hypofunction on interneurons → disinhibition.
   - **GABA:** reduced in cortex (interneurons are dysfunctional). This contributes to gamma-band abnormalities.
   - **Serotonin:** altered — particularly 5-HT2A receptors. Most atypical antipsychotics block 5-HT2A.
   - **Acetylcholine:** some role in cognitive symptoms.
   - **BDNF:** reduced. Impaired neurodevelopment and plasticity.
7. **The brain regions involved.**
   - **Striatum (caudate, putamen):** excess dopamine → positive symptoms. Increased presynaptic dopamine in caudate (Howes et al., PET studies).
   - **Prefrontal cortex (PFC):** deficient dopamine → negative, cognitive. Hypofrontality (reduced PFC activity during working memory tasks).
   - **Hippocampus:** reduced volume (esp. CA1). Hyperactivity in prodrome. Source of "noise" that may drive dopamine dysregulation.
   - **Thalamus:** abnormal connectivity with cortex. Sensory gating deficits.
   - **Amygdala:** altered emotional processing.
8. **The symptoms, explained.**
   - **Auditory hallucinations:** likely arise from *internal speech* being misattributed to external source. The brain's prediction system (predictive coding) is over-generating predictions. The corollary discharge (which normally tags self-generated speech) is impaired.
   - **Delusions:** false beliefs held with absolute conviction. May arise from *aberrant salience* — dopamine inappropriately marks irrelevant stimuli as important. The patient builds an explanatory narrative to make sense of the salience.
   - **Disorganized thought:** loose associations, tangentiality, word salad. May reflect *disrupted predictive coding* — the brain cannot generate coherent predictions.
   - **Negative symptoms:** avolition reflects *mesocortical* dopamine deficiency → reduced motivation, drive.
   - **Cognitive symptoms:** working memory deficits reflect *prefrontal* dysfunction — the PFC cannot maintain task-relevant information.
9. **The neuroinflammatory component.** Recent evidence: schizophrenia is associated with *microglial activation* (especially in prodrome and first episode). Maternal immune activation (MIA) in rodents produces offspring with schizophrenia-like behaviors. This connects the neurodevelopmental and neuroinflammatory hypotheses.
10. **The genetic architecture.** Heritability ~70-80%. But:
    - No single "schizophrenia gene" — it's highly polygenic.
    - GWAS: 100+ risk loci. Each has tiny effect. Combined *polygenic risk score* explains ~7% of variance.
    - Common variants in *COMT, DISC1, NRG1, GRM3, GRIN2A, CACNA1C*, and many others.
    - Rare *de novo* CNVs (copy number variants) at 22q11.2 (DiGeorge syndrome), 16p11.2, 15q11.2, etc. — high penetrance.
    - Concordance in monozygotic twins: ~50%. Even with identical genomes, ~50% of twins are *discordant* — environment matters.
11. **Diagnosis.** Clinical — based on DSM-5 or ICD-11 criteria. Requires:
    - Two or more of: delusions, hallucinations, disorganized speech, grossly disorganized/catatonic behavior, negative symptoms.
    - At least one must be delusions, hallucinations, or disorganized speech.
    - Continuous signs for ≥6 months (including prodromal/residual).
    - Significant social/occupational dysfunction.
    - No other diagnosis (substance-induced, medical condition) better explains.
12. **Treatment.**
    - **Antipsychotics (dopamine D2 antagonists):**
      - **First-gen (typical):** haloperidol, chlorpromazine. Effective for *positive* symptoms. Severe *extrapyramidal* side effects (parkinsonism, tardive dyskinesia).
      - **Second-gen (atypical):** risperidone, olanzapine, clozapine. Effective for positive symptoms; *less* EPS. Side effects: weight gain, metabolic syndrome, sedation.
      - **Clozapine:** the *most effective* antipsychotic. Reserved for *treatment-resistant* schizophrenia. Risk of agranulocytosis (lowered white blood cells) requires regular blood monitoring.
    - **Psychosocial:** cognitive behavioral therapy for psychosis (CBTp), family therapy, supported employment, cognitive remediation, social skills training.
    - **Early intervention:** in the *prodromal* phase or first episode. Reduces long-term disability.
    - **No cure:** current treatments are *symptomatic*. They do not reverse the underlying neurodevelopmental abnormality.
13. **The dopamine paradox.** Why do D2 antagonists *both* treat positive symptoms *and* cause negative symptoms (apathy, flat affect)? Because the same D2 receptors are in both pathways. The solution: *partial* agonists (aripiprazole, brexpiprazole) — they reduce D2 signaling when it's too high but don't reduce it below normal. Or: *targeted* D2 antagonists (limbic-selective). Not yet achieved.
14. **The future.**
    - **Targeted D1 agonists** (positive symptoms, no negative side effects).
    - **Glycine site agonists** (boost NMDA function).
    - **Trace amine-associated receptor (TAAR1) agonists** (SEP-363856) — non-dopaminergic, promising in trials.
    - **Anti-inflammatory:** minocycline, aspirin — mixed results so far.
    - **Neurogenesis:** stimulate BDNF, hippocampal neurogenesis.
    - **AI-assisted diagnosis:** machine learning on speech patterns, facial movements, social media — can detect prodrome years before clinical diagnosis.
    - **Digital phenotyping:** smartphones passively monitor behavior — sleep, movement, social activity — to predict relapse.

**Real-life analogy:** Schizophrenia is like a *radio that has lost its tuning*. The signal is there, but it's noisy, distorted, and full of static. The brain's prediction system (predictive coding) is generating false predictions (hallucinations), assigning false salience (delusions), and failing to organize thought (disorganization). Antipsychotics are like a *noise filter* — they reduce the noise (positive symptoms) but also reduce the signal (cognitive, negative). The challenge: a filter that removes only the noise.

**Tiny numeric example:** A typical patient with schizophrenia:
- Age of onset: ~21 (men earlier than women)
- Lifetime prevalence: ~1%
- Annual incidence: ~0.02% per year
- Brain volume: ~3% smaller than controls (esp. hippocampus, cortex)
- Striatal dopamine synthesis: ~14% higher than controls
- Cortical thickness: reduced in prefrontal and temporal regions
- Hippocampal volume: reduced by ~4% (CA1 most affected)
- Lateral ventricle size: increased
- Genetic contribution: ~80% (twin studies)
- Antipsychotic efficacy: ~60% respond to first drug; ~30% are treatment-resistant
- Clozapine response rate: ~30-60% in treatment-resistant patients
- Life expectancy: reduced by 10-20 years (suicide, cardiovascular disease)
- Suicide rate: ~5% lifetime risk
- Recovery: ~50% have significant long-term improvement; ~25% recover fully

**Common confusion:**

- No. "Schizophrenia = split personality." No. *Schizo* = split, *phrenia* = mind. It refers to a *split* between thought and emotion, or between perception and reality. *Dissociative identity disorder* (DID) is "split personality" and is a different disorder.
- No. "Schizophrenics are violent." Mostly no. People with schizophrenia are *more* likely to be *victims* of violence than perpetrators. Risk of violence is slightly elevated, mostly due to comorbid substance abuse, paranoia, or command hallucinations.
- No. "Antipsychotics cure schizophrenia." No. They treat *symptoms* — especially positive symptoms. ~30% of patients have *negative* or *cognitive* symptoms that don't respond to current drugs. There is no cure.
- No. "Schizophrenia is caused by bad parenting." No. The *refrigerator mother* hypothesis (Bettelheim, 1950s) is *discredited*. Parenting affects *course*, not *cause*. The disorder is neurodevelopmental.
- No. "If you have hallucinations, you have schizophrenia." No. Hallucinations occur in many disorders — Parkinson's (visual), Lewy body dementia, alcohol withdrawal, drug intoxication, sleep deprivation, PTSD, grief. Diagnosis requires a *pattern* of symptoms over time.
- No. "Schizophrenia = psychosis." No. Schizophrenia is *one* cause of psychosis. Psychosis (loss of contact with reality) can occur in many disorders — bipolar, depression, substance use, medical conditions, etc.

**Key properties:**

- **Chronic:** usually lifelong, with relapses and remissions.
- **Multifactorial:** genetic + environmental + neurochemical + neurodevelopmental.
- **Dopamine dysregulation:** excess subcortical, deficient cortical.
- **Glutamate dysregulation:** NMDA hypofunction.
- **Neurodevelopmental:** onset in adolescence/young adulthood; origins in prenatal development.
- **Three symptom clusters:** positive, negative, cognitive.
- **Highly heritable** but polygenic, with environmental triggers.
- **Treatment:** antipsychotics + psychosocial. No cure.

**Where it appears in technology:**

- **AI for early detection:** speech analysis (subtle semantic anomalies), facial movement analysis, social media behavior. Can predict onset 1-2 years before clinical diagnosis.
- **AI for treatment selection:** predict which antipsychotic will work best for a given patient (pharmacogenomics + ML).
- **AI digital phenotyping:** smartphone-based passive monitoring for relapse prediction.
- **AI for hallucinations:** voice assistants, chatbots, generative AI may provide "voices" to patients — therapeutic or harmful (currently debated).
- **Brain-computer interfaces:** could potentially modulate dopamine release, though this is highly experimental.
