# What Is Depression?

**The Problem:** You have a friend who has stopped going to work. They sleep 14 hours a day. They say nothing matters anymore. They've lost interest in things they used to love. They say they feel empty. You tell them to "snap out of it." They can't. What is happening in their brain? Depression is not sadness. It is a *disease* — a disorder of mood, motivation, energy, and cognition — and it has a real, measurable biology.

**Definition:** *Major depressive disorder* (MDD) is a psychiatric disorder characterized by *persistent low mood* and *anhedonia* (inability to feel pleasure), plus cognitive, behavioral, and physiological symptoms, lasting at least 2 weeks. It is the leading cause of disability worldwide. Lifetime prevalence: ~6-17% (depending on criteria). It is twice as common in women as in men.

**How It Works (Step-by-Step):**

1. **The clinical picture.** The DSM-5 criteria (≥5 of 9 symptoms, ≥2 weeks, including either depressed mood or anhedonia):
   - Depressed mood (subjective or observed)
   - Anhedonia (loss of interest/pleasure in *all* or almost all activities)
   - Significant weight loss/gain or appetite change
   - Insomnia or hypersomnia
   - Psychomotor agitation or retardation
   - Fatigue or loss of energy
   - Feelings of worthlessness or excessive guilt
   - Diminished concentration or indecisiveness
   - Recurrent thoughts of death or suicide
2. **The monoamine hypothesis.** The dominant theory for ~50 years:
   - **Serotonin (5-HT):** low. Most SSRIs (selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors) target this. ~70% of patients have *some* response.
   - **Norepinephrine (NE):** low. SNRIs (serotonin-norepinephrine reuptake inhibitors) target this. Improves energy, motivation.
   - **Dopamine (DA):** low. Bupropion is a DA/NE reuptake inhibitor. Improves motivation, anhedonia.
   - Evidence: tricyclic antidepressants and MAO inhibitors (which raise monoamines) work. Reserpine (which depletes monoamines) causes depression. Tryptophan depletion (lowers serotonin) causes relapse in remitted patients.
3. **The problem with the monoamine hypothesis.** It is *incomplete*:
   - Antidepressants raise monoamines *within hours*, but therapeutic effect takes *weeks*. Why the delay?
   - Many depressed patients have *normal* monoamine levels.
   - The 2022 Moncrieff et al. meta-analysis: no consistent evidence that *low serotonin* causes depression.
   - The hypothesis explains *symptoms* but not *cause*. Why does monoamine dysfunction arise?
4. **The BDNF hypothesis.** Reduced *Brain-Derived Neurotrophic Factor* (BDNF) in the hippocampus and prefrontal cortex. (See `06_signals_from_scratch/what_is_BDNF.md`.)
   - Evidence: post-mortem studies show reduced BDNF in depressed patients. Serum BDNF is low in depression.
   - Antidepressants (SSRIs, ECT, ketamine) *increase* BDNF. Therapeutic effect correlates with BDNF normalization.
   - BDNF Val66Met polymorphism (a common human variant) is associated with depression risk and antidepressant response.
   - The *neurotrophic hypothesis of depression*: depression is caused by impaired *neurotrophic support* — the brain cannot grow, repair, or maintain synapses.
5. **The HPA axis hypothesis.** Depression is associated with *dysregulation of the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis*:
   - Chronic stress → cortisol elevation → *downregulation* of glucocorticoid receptors → *failure of negative feedback* → persistent cortisol elevation.
   - High cortisol is *toxic to the hippocampus* — reduces dendritic branching, neurogenesis, and *volume*. (See Part 6's molecular cascade.)
   - ~50% of depressed patients have *abnormal dexamethasone suppression test* (a marker of HPA dysregulation).
   - Chronic stress in early life is a *major* risk factor — adverse childhood experiences (ACEs) predict adult depression.
6. **The neuroinflammation hypothesis.** ~30-40% of depressed patients show *elevated inflammatory markers* (CRP, IL-6, TNF-α). Inflammation:
   - Activates the HPA axis
   - Reduces serotonin (via IDO, the kynurenine pathway)
   - Reduces BDNF
   - Causes "sickness behavior" (fatigue, anhedonia, social withdrawal) — evolutionarily adaptive for acute infection but maladaptive when chronic
   - Chronic inflammation is a risk factor for depression. *And* depression increases inflammation. (Bidirectional.)
7. **The neuroplasticity hypothesis.** Depression is associated with *reduced neuroplasticity* — the brain's ability to form and modify synapses. Evidence:
   - Hippocampal volume is reduced by ~5% in chronic depression (and recovers with treatment).
   - Prefrontal cortex is thinner in chronic depression.
   - Dendritic spine density is reduced in the prefrontal cortex and hippocampus.
   - Neurogenesis in the dentate gyrus is reduced (and antidepressants may *increase* it).
   - Treatment (antidepressants, ECT, ketamine) *restores* neuroplasticity — this may be the *actual* mechanism, not monoamine elevation.
8. **The glutamate hypothesis.** Recent focus: *NMDA receptor* signaling. Evidence:
   - *Ketamine* (NMDA antagonist) has rapid antidepressant effects — within hours, lasting days. (See below.)
   - *Esketamine* (Spravato) is FDA-approved for treatment-resistant depression.
   - The mechanism: ketamine *increases* glutamate burst → AMPA receptor activation → BDNF release → mTOR activation → *rapid synaptogenesis* (within hours).
   - This is the *fastest* antidepressant known. The classical monoamine drugs take weeks; ketamine works in hours.
9. **The circuit-level view.** Depression involves *circuits*, not just chemicals:
   - **Limbic-cortical dysregulation:** hyperactivity in the *ventral medial prefrontal cortex* (including subgenual anterior cingulate) — the "sadness center." Deep brain stimulation of this region *treats* depression.
   - **Reward circuit:** reduced activity in the *ventral striatum* (nucleus accumbens) and *ventral tegmental area* during reward anticipation — the "anhedonia" circuit.
   - **Default mode network (DMN):** hyperactivity. The DMN is the self-referential network. Overactivity = rumination, self-criticism.
   - **Salience network:** altered. Negative stimuli are weighted more heavily.
10. **The genetic architecture.** Heritability ~40% (lower than schizophrenia or bipolar). GWAS: 100+ risk variants. Polygenic risk score explains ~5% of variance. Key findings:
    - No single "depression gene"
    - Many variants in serotonin, BDNF, circadian rhythm, and synaptic genes
    - Strong environmental component (stress, trauma) — genetics loads the gun, environment pulls the trigger
11. **Treatment — the standard.**
    - **SSRIs** (fluoxetine/Prozac, sertraline, escitalopram): first-line. Block serotonin reuptake. Effective in ~50% of patients. Side effects: sexual dysfunction, GI upset, weight gain, emotional blunting. 4-6 weeks for effect.
    - **SNRIs** (venlafaxine, duloxetine): serotonin + norepinephrine. Slightly more effective for severe depression.
    - **Atypical antidepressants** (bupropion, mirtazapine, trazodone). Bupropion: dopamine/norepinephrine, no sexual side effects, helps with smoking cessation.
    - **Tricyclics** (amitriptyline, nortriptyline): older, more side effects (anticholinergic, cardiac), but *highly effective* when tolerated.
    - **MAO inhibitors** (phenelzine, tranylcypromine): oldest. Reserved for treatment-resistant. Dietary restrictions (no cheese, wine).
    - **Combination/augmentation:** lithium, atypical antipsychotics (aripiprazole, quetiapine), thyroid hormone (T3).
12. **Treatment — novel and emerging.**
    - **Ketamine/esketamine:** rapid (within hours), NMDA antagonist, works in treatment-resistant cases. Risk: dissociation, abuse potential.
    - **Psilocybin:** psychedelic. Rapid and sustained antidepressant effects. FDA breakthrough designation. (See `09_brain_to_mind/what_is_consciousness.md` for context.)
    - **TMS (transcranial magnetic stimulation):** non-invasive brain stimulation over the DLPFC. Effective for treatment-resistant.
    - **ECT (electroconvulsive therapy):** the most effective treatment for severe, psychotic, or treatment-resistant depression. ~80% response. Causes temporary seizures. Memory side effects. Used when nothing else works.
    - **Vagus nerve stimulation (VNS):** chronic stimulation of the left vagus nerve. ~30% response in treatment-resistant.
    - **Deep brain stimulation (DBS):** chronic stimulation of the subcallosal cingulate or VC/VS. ~50% response. Last resort.
13. **Lifestyle interventions — strong evidence.**
    - **Exercise:** the most effective *non-pharmacological* treatment. ~30% reduction in symptoms. Mechanism: BDNF↑, neurogenesis, anti-inflammatory, endorphins. Aerobic (30 min × 3-5/week) is the most studied.
    - **Sleep hygiene:** sleep disturbance is both cause and consequence of depression. Treating sleep helps depression.
    - **Social connection:** isolation worsens depression. Connection helps. The most under-prescribed treatment.
    - **Cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT):** ~50% as effective as medication. Best for moderate depression, especially with anxiety. Combined with medication is *most* effective.
    - **Mindfulness-based cognitive therapy (MBCT):** specifically prevents *relapse* in recurrent depression. ~50% reduction in relapse rates.
14. **The future.**
    - **Precision psychiatry:** using genomics, neuroimaging, and digital phenotyping to *individualize* treatment.
    - **AI-driven drug discovery:** psychedelic derivatives, rapid-acting antidepressants.
    - **Closed-loop brain stimulation:** DBS that responds in real-time to neural signatures of low mood.
    - **AI for monitoring:** smartphone-based mood tracking, social media analysis, voice analysis for relapse prediction.

**Real-life analogy:** Depression is like a *car stuck in mud*. The engine (motivation) is running but the wheels (action) can't get traction. The fuel (neurotransmitters) is partially there. The wheels just keep spinning. Antidepressants are like *pushing the car out of the mud* — they don't fix the car's mechanics, but they get it moving. Therapy (CBT) is like *learning to drive on better roads* — preventing getting stuck again. Exercise is like *reinforcing the road* so the car doesn't sink. The combination is best.

**Tiny numeric example:** Major depression in numbers:
- Global prevalence: ~280 million people (WHO, 2023)
- Lifetime risk: ~6-17% (varies by country)
- Female-to-male ratio: ~2:1
- Mean age of onset: ~25-30
- Annual economic cost (US): ~$300 billion (productivity loss + treatment)
- Suicide rate: ~12 per 100,000 per year globally; ~700,000 deaths/year
- Treatment response: ~50% to first SSRI; ~30% to second; ~30% to treatment-resistant clozapine
- Ketamine response rate: ~70% in treatment-resistant (vs ~30% for typical antidepressant)
- Time to SSRI effect: 4-6 weeks
- Time to ketamine effect: hours
- Hippocampal volume reduction in chronic depression: ~5%
- BDNF reduction in depression: ~30%
- HPA axis abnormal: ~50%
- Inflammatory markers elevated: ~30%
- Placebo response rate in trials: ~30-40% (depression trials have high placebo responses — a real phenomenon)
- Genetic contribution (heritability): ~40%
- Childhood adversity as risk factor: 2-3× risk increase

**Common confusion:**

- No. "Depression is just sadness." No. Sadness is a normal emotion, appropriate to circumstances. Depression is *persistent* (≥2 weeks), *pervasive* (affects all aspects of life), and *disabling*. It is not weakness or a choice.
- No. "Antidepressants are happy pills." No. SSRIs don't make you happy. They *reduce the symptoms of depression* — sadness, anhedonia, rumination, fatigue. They don't change personality or induce euphoria (in normal use).
- No. "Antidepressants change your personality." Mostly no. The exception: *emotional blunting* in ~20% of patients (feels like "I don't care about anything"). This is a side effect; dose reduction or switch can help.
- No. "You should just snap out of it." This is the most harmful misconception. Depression is a *disease* with a *biology*. Telling someone to snap out of it is like telling someone with diabetes to just produce more insulin.
- No. "Depression is caused by a chemical imbalance." Partly. The serotonin hypothesis is *not* fully supported. But monoamine systems are involved, and antidepressants that target them work. The *imbalance* framing is a simplification.
- No. "Suicide is a choice." No. Suicidal ideation in depression is a *symptom* of the disease — like a fever in infection. With treatment, suicidal ideation usually resolves.

**Key properties:**

- **Common:** 1 in 6-7 people experience major depression.
- **Multifactorial:** genetic + environmental + neurochemical + neurodevelopmental + inflammatory.
- **Recurrent:** ~50% of those with one episode have another.
- **Disabling:** leading cause of disability worldwide (WHO).
- **Suicidal:** ~5% lifetime suicide risk. The most dangerous symptom.
- **Treatable:** ~70% respond to first-line treatment. ~80% respond overall.
- **Heterogeneous:** depression is *not one thing* — many subtypes, many mechanisms, many treatments.
- **Biologically grounded:** real brain changes (HPA, BDNF, hippocampus, neuroplasticity).

**Where it appears in technology:**

- **AI for early detection:** smartphone typing patterns, voice features, social media posts — predict depression before clinical diagnosis.
- **AI for treatment selection:** ML models predict which antidepressant will work for a given patient (pharmacogenomics + clinical features).
- **AI digital phenotyping:** passive smartphone monitoring for relapse prediction.
- **AI chatbots (Woebot, Wysa):** evidence-based CBT delivered through conversation. Effective for mild-to-moderate depression.
- **AI companions and crisis support:** AI lines, while imperfect, may provide immediate support when human therapists aren't available.
- **BCI in depression:** closed-loop DBS for treatment-resistant depression (Schoenbaum, Dougherty labs).
- **AI in psychedelic research:** helping identify optimal dosing, set, setting for psilocybin therapy.
