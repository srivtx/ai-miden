# What Is Parkinson's Disease?

**The Problem:** Your grandfather's hands shake. His voice has become soft, almost a whisper. He walks slowly, shuffling his feet, and sometimes freezes mid-step. He has trouble getting out of a chair. He is sad — *very* sad — and you wonder if the sadness is just because of his illness, or part of it. He has Parkinson's disease.

**Definition:** *Parkinson's disease* (PD) is a progressive neurodegenerative disorder characterized by the *death of dopaminergic neurons in the substantia nigra* (a small brainstem nucleus that produces dopamine for the basal ganglia). The result is the classic motor triad of *tremor, bradykinesia, and rigidity* — plus a long list of non-motor symptoms (depression, sleep disturbance, cognitive decline, autonomic dysfunction).

**How It Works (Step-by-Step):**

1. **The macroscopic story.** PD begins in the *substantia nigra pars compacta* (SNc) — a brainstem nucleus whose neurons project to the striatum (caudate + putamen). The SNc neurons produce *dopamine*, the key neurotransmitter for the *direct* basal ganglia pathway. As these neurons die, the basal ganglia can no longer select actions properly. The result: *bradykinesia* (slow movement), *rigidity* (stiffness), *tremor* (rhythmic shaking), and *postural instability* (balance problems).
2. **The microscopic story — α-synuclein.** PD is a *synucleinopathy*. The protein *α-synuclein* is normally found in presynaptic terminals. In PD, it *misfolds* into β-sheet conformation, aggregates into *oligomers* and *fibrils*, and forms *Lewy bodies* — dense, round inclusions inside neurons. Lewy bodies are *toxic* — they disrupt cellular function and trigger cell death.
3. **The progression — Braak staging.** Heiko Braak (2003) proposed a 6-stage model:
   - Stages 1-2: pathology in the *dorsal motor nucleus of the vagus* and *olfactory bulb*. Non-motor symptoms (loss of smell, constipation, REM sleep behavior disorder) appear.
   - Stages 3-4: pathology reaches the *substantia nigra*. Motor symptoms appear.
   - Stages 5-6: pathology reaches the *cortex*. Cognitive decline and dementia appear.
4. **The genetic forms.** ~5-10% of PD cases are *familial*:
   - **SNCA:** encodes α-synuclein itself. Duplications/triplications cause early-onset PD.
   - **LRRK2:** the most common genetic cause. ~1-2% of all PD, ~40% of familial PD.
   - **Parkin (PRKN), PINK1, DJ-1:** cause early-onset autosomal recessive PD. Mitochondrial dysfunction.
   - **GBA:** risk gene. Also causes Gaucher disease when biallelic.
5. **The environmental factors.**
   - **MPTP:** a synthetic heroin contaminant that *selectively* kills dopaminergic neurons (Langston, 1983). Proved that environmental toxins can cause PD.
   - **Pesticides (paraquat, rotenone, glyphosate):** correlated with PD risk. Mitochondrial toxins.
   - **Trichloroethylene (TCE):** industrial solvent. EPA classifies as a probable human carcinogen. Linked to PD.
   - **Head injury:** traumatic brain injury increases PD risk.
   - **Rural living, well-water consumption:** risk factors, possibly via pesticides.
6. **The cellular mechanism — why do SNc neurons die?**
   - **α-synuclein aggregation:** disrupts protein homeostasis, mitochondrial function, synaptic transmission.
   - **Mitochondrial dysfunction:** complex I impairment, reduced ATP, increased ROS.
   - **Calcium dyshomeostasis:** SNc neurons have *pacemaking* activity (L-type Ca²⁺ channels), leading to high intracellular Ca²⁺ → mitochondrial stress.
   - **Iron accumulation:** SNc has high iron content. Iron + α-synuclein → oxidative stress.
   - **Neuroinflammation:** activated microglia release cytokines → chronic damage.
7. **The basal ganglia imbalance.** (See `03_architecture/what_is_basal_ganglia.md`.) The direct pathway (D1, "go") and indirect pathway (D2, "stop") normally balance. With dopamine loss:
   - Direct pathway: *underactive* → less movement initiation → bradykinesia
   - Indirect pathway: *overactive* (no D2 inhibition) → more movement suppression → rigidity
   - Tremor: rhythmic firing of the subthalamic nucleus + thalamus (oscillatory network)
8. **The non-motor symptoms.** PD is *not* just a movement disorder. ~all patients have non-motor symptoms:
   - **Depression:** ~40% — *more* common than in any other chronic disease
   - **REM sleep behavior disorder:** ~50% — patients act out dreams; often *precedes* motor symptoms by years
   - **Loss of smell (anosmia):** ~90% — often the *first* symptom, years before motor
   - **Constipation:** ~50% — gut is affected early (Braak stage 1-2)
   - **Cognitive decline:** ~30% develop dementia (PD dementia)
   - **Autonomic dysfunction:** orthostatic hypotension, urinary problems, sexual dysfunction
   - **Pain:** ~40% — often under-recognized
9. **The role of dopamine in motivation.** PD is also a *motivational* disorder. The mesolimbic dopamine system (VTA → nucleus accumbens) is the *reward* and *wanting* system. PD patients have *apathy* — they know what they should do but lack the *drive* to do it. This is not laziness; it is *neurochemical*. (See `04_systems/what_is_reward.md`.)
10. **The role of BDNF.** PD patients have *reduced BDNF* in the striatum and substantia nigra. This impairs neuronal survival and *growth*. The dopamine neurons of SNc are particularly dependent on *GDNF* (glial cell line-derived neurotrophic factor), a related growth factor. Trials of GDNF delivery in PD are ongoing.
11. **Treatment — L-DOPA.** The mainstay: *levodopa* (L-DOPA, the dopamine precursor). It crosses the blood-brain barrier (dopamine itself doesn't). Once in the brain, it's converted to dopamine by *aromatic L-amino acid decarboxylase* (AADC). Combined with *carbidopa* (peripheral AADC inhibitor) to reduce side effects. L-DOPA dramatically improves motor symptoms — patients who couldn't move can suddenly walk. It is *the* miracle drug of neurology.
12. **Treatment — other drugs.**
   - **Dopamine agonists** (pramipexole, ropinirole): directly stimulate dopamine receptors. Longer half-life than L-DOPA. Cause hallucinations, impulse control disorders (pathological gambling, hypersexuality).
   - **MAO-B inhibitors** (selegiline, rasagiline, safinamide): block dopamine breakdown. Mild symptomatic benefit. May be *disease-modifying* (controversial).
   - **COMT inhibitors** (entacapone, tolcapone): block peripheral dopamine breakdown. Extend L-DOPA half-life.
   - **Anticholinergics** (trihexyphenidyl): reduce tremor. Cognitive side effects.
13. **Deep brain stimulation (DBS).** When drugs fail, electrodes implanted in the *subthalamic nucleus* (STN) or *globus pallidus interna* (GPi) deliver continuous high-frequency stimulation. Dramatic benefit: 50-70% reduction in motor symptoms, reduced medication. Mechanism: disrupts pathological oscillations. *Cannot* slow disease progression.
14. **The future.**
    - **α-synuclein immunotherapy:** antibodies that clear α-synuclein. Several in trials.
    - **GDNF delivery:** direct infusion of growth factor into the brain.
    - **Cell therapy:** transplantation of dopamine neurons (induced pluripotent stem cells).
    - **Gene therapy:** AAV-mediated delivery of GAD (to convert excitatory to inhibitory in STN) — approved in some countries.
    - **Exercise:** aerobic exercise reduces PD progression. Mechanism: BDNF↑, neurogenesis, anti-inflammatory.

**Real-life analogy:** PD is like a *car with failing fuel injection*. The fuel (dopamine) is critical for movement. As the fuel system fails, the car sputters, hesitates, eventually can't move smoothly. L-DOPA is like adding more fuel — it works, but doesn't fix the underlying fuel system. DBS is like *electronically* controlling the engine — bypassing the broken system with a computer. The disease is the broken system; treatments are *workarounds*.

**Tiny numeric example:** A typical PD patient at diagnosis:
- ~50-80% of SNc dopaminergic neurons have *already died* (by the time of motor symptoms)
- ~5-10% of striatal dopamine remains
- Brain has lost ~7% of weight
- Tremor frequency: 4-6 Hz (the classic "pill-rolling" tremor)
- Gait: shuffling, short steps, decreased arm swing
- Voice: hypophonia (soft), monotonic
- L-DOPA response: dramatic (50% improvement in motor scores)
- L-DOPA complications: dyskinesias (involuntary movements) after 5-10 years in ~50% of patients
- Life expectancy: ~12-15 years after diagnosis
- Prevalence: ~1% over 60, ~3% over 80

**Common confusion:**

- No. "Parkinson's is just tremor." No. Tremor is the *visible* symptom but bradykinesia and rigidity are often more disabling. Many PD patients do *not* have prominent tremor (the "PIGD" subtype).
- No. "L-DOPA stops working after a few years." No. L-DOPA continues to work, but *motor fluctuations* and *dyskinesias* develop. The "on-off" phenomenon (sudden loss of effect) and dyskinesias are *complications*, not loss of efficacy. The solution: more frequent dosing, deep brain stimulation.
- No. "PD is just a movement disorder." No. ~all patients have non-motor symptoms. Depression, dementia, autonomic dysfunction are often more disabling than motor symptoms.
- No. "PD is genetic." Mostly *sporadic* (~90-95%). Genetic forms are informative for understanding but uncommon.
- No. "DBS is a last resort." No. DBS is effective *earlier* than typically used. Modern guidelines recommend earlier DBS. It improves quality of life for many years.
- No. "Tremor = PD." No. *Essential tremor* (much more common) is also a tremor disorder. PD tremor is *resting* tremor (4-6 Hz); essential tremor is *postural/action* tremor (4-12 Hz, usually faster, often with family history).

**Key properties:**

- **Progressive:** symptoms worsen over years.
- **Dopaminergic:** loss of SNc dopaminergic neurons.
- **Synucleinopathy:** α-synuclein aggregates (Lewy bodies).
- **Motor + non-motor:** tremor, bradykinesia, rigidity, depression, dementia, sleep, autonomic.
- **Pre-motor phase:** loss of smell, REM sleep behavior disorder, constipation — years before motor symptoms.
- **Treatable but not curable:** L-DOPA, DBS help dramatically; α-synuclein immunotherapy may modify disease.
- **Common:** ~1% over 60, ~3% over 80.
- **Age-related:** mean onset ~60.

**Where it appears in technology:**

- **AI-assisted diagnosis:** machine learning on gait (via wearable sensors or video) detects early PD. Validated in clinical trials.
- **AI for DBS programming:** adaptive DBS adjusts stimulation in real-time based on neural signals. Closed-loop systems.
- **AI for drug discovery:** targets α-synuclein aggregation, mitochondrial rescue, neurotrophic factors.
- **Wearable technology:** smartwatches detect tremor, dyskinesia, sleep disturbance. Provide objective outcome measures.
- **AI assistants:** LLMs help PD patients with memory, scheduling, voice amplification.
