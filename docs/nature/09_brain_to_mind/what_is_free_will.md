# What Is Free Will?

**The Problem:** You feel like you make choices. You *decided* to read this file. But neuroscience shows that your brain's motor preparation (the "readiness potential," or Bereitschaftspotential) begins *before* you are consciously aware of deciding. Does this mean free will is an illusion? Or is the neuroscience telling us something subtler?

**Definition:** *Free will* is the capacity to make choices that are (a) *up to you* (not determined by forces outside your control), and (b) *causally effective* (your choices actually cause your actions). The philosophical problem: how can free will exist in a deterministic physical universe? The neuroscience question: when does a "decision" actually occur?

**How It Works (Step-by-Step):**

1. **The classical view.** Descartes: mind and body are separate substances. Mind (res cogitans) is non-physical and free. Body (res extensa) is physical and deterministic. They interact in the pineal gland. (Most neuroscientists reject this dualism.)
2. **The deterministic view.** Laplace: given complete knowledge of the universe's state, the future is determined. The brain is a physical system. Its behavior is determined by prior states. There is no "room" for free will.
3. **Libet's experiments (1983).** Benjamin Libet measured the *readiness potential* (RP) — a slow rise in EEG signal over motor cortex that precedes voluntary movement. He found:
   - RP onset: ~550 ms before the movement
   - Conscious "decision" to move (reported by subjects): ~200 ms before the movement
   - The RP *precedes* the conscious decision by ~350 ms
4. **The interpretation.** "Conscious will" lags the brain's preparation to act. This is taken (by some) as evidence that free will is an *illusion* — the decision is made unconsciously, and consciousness comes in afterward to "claim" it.
5. **The counter-arguments.** Libet's experiment has been criticized:
   - **The "subjective" decision is not the same as the neural decision.** Subjective reports of *when* you decided may be inaccurate.
   - **RP is not specific to the chosen action.** The RP is present even for *non-chosen* actions in a free-choice paradigm. It reflects general preparation, not the specific decision.
   - **Subjects could veto.** Libet himself noted that subjects could *cancel* a movement even after the RP started. The "veto window" is ~100-200 ms.
   - **Readiness potential is not a "decision."** It is a *preparation*. The decision might be earlier or later.
6. **Modern experiments.** Haggard (2008), Soon et al. (2008), and others have refined Libet's work:
   - **fMRI-based prediction:** activity in parietal and prefrontal cortex can predict a binary choice *seconds* before the subject reports deciding. But this is at *chance levels above 60%* — far from deterministic.
   - **The "pre-reflective" sense of agency:** the feeling of "I did that" comes *before* the action is completed, but *after* the decision is made. It is a *monitoring* signal, not the decision itself.
7. **The compatibilist view.** Most modern philosophers are *compatibilists*: free will is compatible with determinism. The argument:
   - A determined action is "free" if it comes from the right *internal* causes — your desires, beliefs, deliberation, character.
   - A determined action is *not* free if it comes from *external* coercion — brainwashing, threats, manipulation.
   - The neuroscience is consistent with compatibilism: the brain's processes *are* determined, but they are *your* processes, not someone else's. Your decision was determined by *your* character and deliberation.
8. **The libertarian view.** Some philosophers (Kane, O'Connor) hold that free will requires *genuine indeterminism* — quantum randomness or otherwise. The brain's micro-physics provides the indeterminism; the macro-level decision is *amplified* from it.
9. **The neuroscience of responsibility.** Even if determinism is true, the legal and social system needs *responsibility*. Compatibilism provides a framework: people are responsible if their decisions are *responsive to reasons* (not coerced), *reflective* (not impulsive), and *informed* (not deceived). The brain's *prefrontal* function is critical here — patients with prefrontal damage may be *unable* to act on reasons.
10. **The illusionist view.** Some philosophers (Derk Pereboom, Galen Strawson) argue that even compatibilist free will is incoherent. If your decisions are determined by prior causes, you *could not* have done otherwise, even if those causes were internal. This is the "hard incompatibilism" position.

**Real-life analogy:** Free will is like *authorship*. A novel is *determined* by the words on the page, the structure of the language, the chemistry of the ink. But the *author* is the one who wrote it. The book's existence is determined, but the author is *responsible*. Compatibilism says: your decisions are determined by *your* brain, your history, your character. That makes them *yours* — not determined by something else. You are the *author* of your actions, even if the process is deterministic.

**Tiny numeric example:** A typical reaction-time experiment:
- Stimulus presented at T = 0
- Readout from single neurons in parietal cortex (LIP) predicts the choice at T ≈ 600-1000 ms before the response
- fMRI patterns in frontopolar cortex predict at T ≈ 4-10 seconds before the response
- The reported conscious decision is at T ≈ 200 ms before the response
- The motor response is at T ≈ 300-500 ms after the stimulus

The "neural prediction" is 4-10 seconds before the conscious decision. Does this mean the decision is *unconscious*? Or is it that the *brain* is preparing, but the *decision* (a specific commitment) happens later? The literature is split.

**Common confusion:**

- No. "Libet proved free will is an illusion." No. Libet's experiment shows the *readiness potential* precedes the conscious report. It does *not* show that the decision is unconscious — the decision could be elsewhere in the brain, or the subjective report could be inaccurate.
- No. "The brain is deterministic, so there's no free will." This conflates determinism with predictability. The brain is *probabilistic* (stochastic noise at every level). Even a deterministic system can be unpredictable in practice.
- No. "If I have free will, I could have done otherwise." This is the "alternative possibilities" condition. Some compatibilists deny it — they say freedom is about *how* you decided, not *whether* you could have decided otherwise.
- No. "Neuroscience has disproved moral responsibility." No. Compatibilist accounts of responsibility do not require libertarian free will. The legal and moral system can survive.
- No. "Quantum mechanics gives us free will." Possibly, but the decoherence time in the brain is ~10⁻¹³ seconds — too fast to influence a decision that takes ~10⁻¹-10⁰ seconds. Quantum effects in the brain are unlikely to provide macroscopic indeterminism. (Penrose & Hameroff disagree, but the consensus is against them.)
- No. "I have no free will because I am my brain." This is the "no-one-is-home" view. Most people, and most legal systems, reject it. We hold people responsible for actions *they chose*, even if those choices are determined by their brains.

**Key properties:**

- **Philosophical problem:** Free will vs. determinism is one of the oldest problems in philosophy.
- **Empirically testable:** Libet's experiment and successors are real neuroscience.
- **Not resolved:** The question of whether free will "really exists" is open.
- **Compatibilism is dominant:** Most modern philosophers and neuroscientists accept compatibilism.
- **Responsibility is independent:** Legal and moral responsibility can survive determinism.
- **Brain correlates:** Prefrontal cortex is critical for reflective choice.

**Where it appears in technology:**

- **AI agency:** does an AI have "free will"? Most say no — AI's actions are determined by its training, weights, and inputs. But the question becomes interesting for *agentic* AI.
- **Legal liability:** who is responsible when an AI makes a harmful decision? The developer? The user? The AI itself? These questions are increasingly urgent.
- **Brain stimulation:** deep brain stimulation (DBS) can alter mood, decision-making, even personality. The "freedom" of a DBS patient's decisions is philosophically murky.
- **Addiction and responsibility:** a heroin addict's "decision" to use is influenced by neurochemistry. Does that reduce their responsibility? Compatibilism: yes, but the legal and moral system still has to address it.
- **Predictive neuroscience:** if we can predict a choice seconds before the subject decides, does that change how we think about responsibility? The legal system is grappling with this.
