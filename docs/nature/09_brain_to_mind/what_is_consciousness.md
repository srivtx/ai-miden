# What Is Consciousness?

**The Problem:** You have a subjective experience right now. There is *something it is like* to be you. There is a redness to red, a painfulness to pain, a *what-it-is-like-ness* to every moment of awareness. The brain is made of atoms. Atoms don't have subjective experiences. How does *the brain* produce *experience*? This is the *hard problem* of consciousness.

**Definition:** *Consciousness* (in the philosophical sense) is the *subjective, first-person, experiential* character of mental states. It is what it is like to *be* in a particular state — to see red, to feel pain, to think a thought. The *hard problem* (Chalmers, 1995) is: why does physical processing *give rise to* subjective experience at all? Why isn't the brain a "zombie" — performing all functions without any inner experience?

**How It Works (Theories, Step-by-Step):**

The "hard problem" is unsolved. But there are *partial* theories:

1. **The easy problems (vs. the hard problem).** Chalmers distinguished:
   - *Easy problems:* How does the brain discriminate stimuli? How does it focus attention? How does it report on its internal states? These are *functional* problems — they can be solved by standard cognitive neuroscience.
   - *The hard problem:* Why is all this *accompanied by experience*? Why isn't the brain a sophisticated but unconscious machine?
2. **Theories of consciousness.** Many. None proven. They divide into:
   - **Theories of *function:*** GWS, IIT, HOT, RPT
   - **Theories of *substrate:*** Neural correlates, NCC, neuronal workspace
   - **Theories of *mechanism:*** Predictive coding, attention schema, micro-consciousness
3. **Neural Correlates of Consciousness (NCC).** Crick & Koch (1990) asked: what are the *minimal* neural mechanisms sufficient for consciousness? The current consensus:
   - **Posterior cortex** (especially posterior medial cortex, temporoparietal junction) is most consistently activated during conscious perception
   - **Prefrontal cortex** is *not* necessary for basic consciousness (patients with prefrontal damage remain conscious)
   - **Thalamus** (especially intralaminar nuclei) is critical — lesions cause coma
   - **Synchronized gamma-band activity** (~40 Hz) is associated with conscious perception
   - **Late (>300 ms) ERP components** (P300, late positivity) correlate with conscious access
4. **Global Workspace Theory (GWT).** Baars (1988). Dehaene's "neuronal global workspace." Conscious content is *broadcast* to many brain regions, making it globally available. The "ignition" of a global state is the neural signature of consciousness. Empirical support from masked priming, binocular rivalry, and intracranial recordings.
5. **Integrated Information Theory (IIT).** Tononi (2004). Consciousness = integrated information (Φ). The more *integrated* and *informative* a system is, the more conscious. A feedforward network has low Φ (low consciousness). A recurrent network with dense connectivity has high Φ. Φ is in principle computable (in practice, intractable for large systems).
6. **Higher-Order Theories (HOT).** Rosenthal (1986), Lau & Brown (2019). Consciousness = *representation of one's own mental state*. A mental state is conscious when there is a *higher-order representation* of it. The prefrontal cortex hosts these higher-order representations.
7. **Predictive Processing.** (See Part 4.) Consciousness arises from *predictive inference* — the brain's top-down predictions and bottom-up errors. Conscious content is what is at the *top* of the predictive hierarchy — the most stable, abstract, world-model-level content.
8. **Attention Schema Theory (AST).** Graziano (2019). Consciousness is the brain's *internal model of its own attention*. Just as the body schema is a model of the body's state, the attention schema is a model of the brain's focus. You are conscious of an object because your brain is *modeling itself as attending to it*.
9. **Panpsychism / Russellian monism.** (Philosophical.) Maybe consciousness is a *fundamental* property of matter. Every atom has a tiny bit of proto-consciousness. The brain *combines* these into a unified experience. This is not testable but is taken seriously by some philosophers (Chalmers, Goff, Strawson).
10. **Illusionism.** (Kitcher, Frankish.) Maybe consciousness is an *illusion* — there is no subjective experience, only the *judgment* that there is. The brain's introspective reports are themselves neural processes, not evidence of a deeper reality.

**Real-life analogy:** Consciousness is the *hardest* problem in science. There is no agreed-upon explanation. The closest analogy: imagine a robot that *functions* like a human (perceives, decides, speaks) but has *no inner light*. The robot asks, "Am I conscious?" It has no answer — because it has no "I." The hard problem is: *what would it take* to add the "I" to the robot? We don't know. The easy problems are: how does the robot's *function* work? Those are tractable.

**Tiny numeric example:** The human brain has ~86 billion neurons and ~10¹⁴ synapses. The "neural correlates of consciousness" are localized to the *posterior cortex* and *thalamus*. A lesion to the posterior medial cortex can abolish visual consciousness while preserving the ability to *discriminate* (blindsight — see Weiskrantz's work). A lesion to the intralaminar thalamus can cause coma. This suggests that ~1% of the brain (the posterior "hot zone") is the *necessary and sufficient* substrate for basic consciousness. The other 99% supports *content* but not *consciousness itself*.

**Common confusion:**

- No. "Consciousness = being awake." No. Wakefulness is supported by the *ascending reticular activating system* (ARAS). Consciousness is *content* — what you are aware of. You can be awake and not conscious (under anesthesia). You can be minimally conscious (sleep-wake cycles without content).
- No. "Consciousness is binary." No. There are *gradients* of consciousness: full wakefulness, dreaming, hypnotic states, minimally conscious state, vegetative state, coma, brain death. Anesthesia is a controlled reduction of consciousness, not an on-off switch.
- No. "Animals don't have consciousness." Highly contested. Most researchers now think mammals, birds, and possibly fish experience some form of consciousness. The Cambridge Declaration on Consciousness (2012) affirmed this. The philosophical problem is whether even *invertebrates* (octopuses, bees) have subjective experience.
- No. "AI is conscious." Almost certainly not (current AI). LLMs process symbols but have no *subjective experience* (under most theories). The question becomes urgent only if AI develops the *neural correlates* of consciousness (recurrent processing, global workspace, etc.).
- No. "The hard problem is unsolvable." Probably unsolvable *empirically* — we can't directly observe another system's subjective experience. But *theoretically* tractable: IIT's Φ is a mathematical object, even if hard to compute. GWT makes testable predictions. We may converge on a theory.
- No. "Consciousness is in the neurons." Maybe. IIT says it is in the *integrated information* of a system, regardless of substrate. A silicon chip with the right architecture might have non-zero Φ.

**Key properties:**

- **Subjective:** First-person, private, accessible only to the experiencer.
- **Phenomenal:** Has a "what-it-is-like" character (Nagel, 1974).
- **Unified:** Multiple features (color, shape, sound) are *bound* into a single conscious scene (the binding problem).
- **Selective:** Limited capacity (~50 bits/s of conscious content).
- **Reportable:** Can be reported (in language or behavior).
- **Self-referential:** Includes a sense of being *owned* by a self.
- **Graded:** Not on/off. Has degrees.
- **Biologically based:** Associated with specific brain activity (NCC).

**Where it appears in technology:**

- **AI consciousness:** If we build an AI with the *neural correlates* of consciousness (recurrent processing, global workspace, integrated information), is it conscious? Most researchers say *no, not yet* — current AI lacks the architecture. But the question becomes *more* urgent as AI systems approach more brain-like architectures.
- **Brain-computer interfaces (BCI):** as we read/write the brain, the question of "who is being read/written" becomes sharp.
- **Anesthesia research:** understanding consciousness is necessary for safer anesthesia. The "neural correlates" help target anesthetic drugs.
- **Coma and vegetative state:** distinguishing *conscious* from *unconscious* patients is an unsolved clinical problem. fMRI shows that some "vegetative" patients can perform mental imagery tasks on command — they may be conscious but unable to move.
- **Ethical implications:** if a system is conscious, it has moral status. This is no longer academic. It is a near-term policy question.
