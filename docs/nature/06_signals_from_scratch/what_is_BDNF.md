# What Is BDNF (Brain-Derived Neurotrophic Factor)?

**The Problem:** Activity changes synaptic strength (LTP, LTD). But how does *long-term* plasticity happen? How does a brief burst of activity turn into a *new synapse*? The answer is **growth factors** — proteins released by active neurons that trigger the growth, survival, and differentiation of other neurons. The most important is **BDNF**.

**Definition:** *Brain-Derived Neurotrophic Factor* (BDNF) is a small protein (~13 kDa) of the *neurotrophin* family. It is released by active neurons, binds to *TrkB* receptors on target neurons, and triggers *growth, survival, synaptogenesis, and plasticity*. BDNF is the *executor* of activity-dependent change: it turns "this neuron fired" into "this synapse should grow."

**How It Works (Step-by-Step):**

1. **Synthesis.** BDNF is encoded by the *BDNF* gene. The gene has 8 alternative promoters, producing transcripts with different 5' exons. Promoter IV is *activity-dependent* — its transcription is triggered by Ca²⁺ influx, cAMP, and CREB phosphorylation. So *active* neurons produce more BDNF.
2. **Secretion.** BDNF is synthesized in the endoplasmic reticulum, packaged into *dense-core vesicles*, and secreted in an *activity-dependent* manner. Secretion happens at both presynaptic and postsynaptic sites, in response to high-frequency firing or strong depolarization. Importantly, BDNF is secreted as a *pro-form* (proBDNF), which is cleaved extracellularly into mature BDNF.
3. **Two receptors.** BDNF binds two receptors:
   - **TrkB** (tropomyosin receptor kinase B): the high-affinity receptor. TrkB is a receptor tyrosine kinase — when BDNF binds, it dimerizes and *autophosphorylates*, activating downstream signaling (PI3K, MAPK, PLCγ).
   - **p75NTR** (p75 neurotrophin receptor): the low-affinity receptor. p75NTR can signal apoptosis (cell death) or survival, depending on context. ProBDNF preferentially binds p75NTR, which mediates *synapse elimination* (the opposite of BDNF's usual effect).
4. **TrkB signaling.** Activated TrkB triggers three major cascades:
   - **PI3K/Akt:** promotes *cell survival* (inhibits pro-apoptotic proteins), *protein synthesis* (via mTOR), and *AMPA receptor insertion* into the postsynaptic density.
   - **MAPK/ERK:** promotes *gene transcription* (via CREB) and *cell differentiation* (growth, new synapses).
   - **PLCγ:** produces IP3 and DAG, leading to *Ca²⁺ release* from ER — further amplifying the Ca²⁺ signal.
5. **Effects on synapses.** BDNF, via TrkB, promotes:
   - **LTP enhancement:** BDNF applied to hippocampal slices enhances LTP. BDNF blockers impair LTP.
   - **AMPA receptor insertion:** TrkB → PI3K → Akt → GluA1 phosphorylation → AMPA receptor insertion in the postsynaptic density.
   - **Spine growth:** TrkB → MAPK → gene expression → new actin and PSD-95 synthesis → spine enlargement.
   - **Presynaptic enhancement:** BDNF increases presynaptic release probability and vesicle pool size.
   - **Synaptogenesis:** BDNF promotes the formation of new synapses, especially in development.
6. **BDNF and exercise.** Physical exercise (aerobic, voluntary) *robustly* increases BDNF expression in hippocampus and cortex — by 2-3×. This is the molecular basis of *exercise-induced neurogenesis* (in the dentate gyrus) and *exercise-induced cognitive improvement*. Exercise may protect against Alzheimer's, depression, and age-related cognitive decline — at least partly via BDNF.
7. **BDNF and depression.** The *neurotrophic hypothesis of depression* states that depression is associated with reduced BDNF. Antidepressants (SSRIs, ketamine, ECT) increase BDNF. The therapeutic lag of SSRIs (4-6 weeks) corresponds to the time it takes to upregulate BDNF and grow new synapses.
8. **The Val66Met polymorphism.** A common human polymorphism in the BDNF gene (valine → methionine at position 66) impairs activity-dependent BDNF secretion. Met66 carriers have:
   - Reduced hippocampal volume
   - Impaired episodic memory
   - Increased risk of depression, anxiety, Alzheimer's
   - Reduced response to exercise-induced cognitive improvement
   This is a *direct* link between a single gene variant and human cognition.
9. **BDNF in development.** BDNF is required for *survival* of many neurons during development. BDNF knockouts are usually *lethal* — they die within weeks of birth. The brain's developmental wiring is partly controlled by BDNF gradients: neurons that receive enough BDNF survive; others undergo apoptosis.
10. **Transport and release.** BDNF mRNA is transported down dendrites to *local* sites, where it can be translated and secreted. This allows *local*, synapse-specific BDNF signaling — the "synapse that fired" gets the BDNF.

**Real-life analogy:** BDNF is like a *fertilizer* for neurons. Active neurons release it. Nearby synapses (and other neurons) absorb it. The fertilized synapses *grow* — more AMPA receptors, larger spines, more presynaptic release sites. Inactive synapses get less fertilizer and *shrivel*. This is how activity-dependent *competitive plasticity* happens: the most active inputs win, the least active lose. BDNF is the *currency* of neural competition.

**Tiny numeric example:** A single active cortical pyramidal neuron can release ~1-10 BDNF molecules per action potential, locally. Over a few minutes of high-frequency stimulation, ~1000-10000 BDNF molecules are released at a single synapse. The local TrkB concentration is ~100-1000 receptors per synapse. Each BDNF-TrkB binding event triggers phosphorylation cascades. Net result: over ~1 hour, the synapse is structurally larger, has more AMPA receptors, and is more likely to fire in the future. Sleep helps consolidate this — sharp-wave ripples in sleep replay active sequences and trigger BDNF-dependent spine growth.

**Common confusion:**

- No. "More BDNF = smarter." No. BDNF is *necessary* but not sufficient. Excess BDNF without patterned activity doesn't make you smarter. BDNF provides the *substrate* for plasticity; activity provides the *instruction*.
- No. "BDNF is a drug." Endogenous BDNF is released by your own neurons. Recombinant BDNF has been tried as a therapeutic (e.g., for ALS, Parkinson's) but it doesn't cross the blood-brain barrier well and has off-target effects.
- No. "Exercise is good because of BDNF." Partly. Exercise also increases neurogenesis, blood flow, insulin sensitivity, BDNF-independent growth factors, and reduces inflammation. BDNF is one of many mechanisms.
- No. "BDNF only acts on TrkB." It also binds p75NTR, with very different effects (apoptosis, synapse elimination). The proBDNF/mature BDNF balance matters.
- No. "All neurotrophins are the same." No. NGF (nerve growth factor) acts on TrkA, mainly in sympathetic and sensory neurons. NT-3 acts on TrkC. NT-4 acts on TrkB (like BDNF). They have overlapping but distinct roles.
- No. "BDNF is only in the brain." No. BDNF is also in skeletal muscle, saliva, prostate, retina. It has roles outside the nervous system.

**Key properties:**

- **Activity-dependent:** Activity upregulates BDNF expression and secretion.
- **Trophic:** Promotes growth, survival, differentiation.
- **Local:** Acts on nearby synapses; can be targeted to specific spines.
- **Bidirectional:** ProBDNF (via p75NTR) causes synapse elimination; mature BDNF (via TrkB) causes synapse growth.
- **TrkB-mediated:** Most effects go through TrkB → PI3K/Akt + MAPK/ERK + PLCγ.
- **Crucial for plasticity:** Without BDNF, LTP is impaired.
- **Developmental:** Required for neuronal survival and wiring in development.
- **Exercise-responsive:** Activity (exercise) increases BDNF 2-3×.

**Where it appears in technology:**

- **Exercise mimetics:** drugs that mimic exercise's effects on BDNF (e.g., 7,8-DHF, a TrkB agonist). Under research as cognitive enhancers.
- **Ketamine** for treatment-resistant depression: rapidly increases BDNF. The fast antidepressant effect (hours) is partly BDNF-dependent.
- **Nootropics:** many "smart drugs" (racetams, etc.) are claimed to work via BDNF. The evidence is mixed.
- **In AI:** the *equivalent* of BDNF in a learning system would be a *growth signal* — a scalar that tells the system "this area is valuable, grow more capacity here." This is what *reward* and *curiosity* signals do in modern RL. The brain uses chemistry (BDNF) to do this. AI uses gradients.
- **In robotics:** embodied agents that learn benefit from "intrinsic motivation" signals — analogous to BDNF. The Curiosity-driven RL agents (Pathak et al., 2017) implement this.
