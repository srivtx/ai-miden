Why it exists
-------------
Location claims are easy to fake. A smartphone can spoof GPS coordinates. A
delivery driver can claim to be at a restaurant while sitting at home. A
ride-share passenger can pretend to be in a city where surge pricing is
lower. Proof of location exists to solve this by creating cryptographic
evidence that a device was physically present at a specific place at a
specific time. It combines hardware measurements, radio signals, and
blockchain attestations to make fake locations expensive and detectable.
Without proof of location, decentralized logistics, gaming, and mapping
services cannot trust spatial data.

Definition
----------
Proof of location is a cryptographic attestation that a device or entity
was at a specific geographic coordinate at a specific time. It is typically
generated by specialized hardware that listens to radio signals, satellite
data, or mesh network beacons and signs a timestamped location claim. Other
nodes in the network verify the claim by checking signal consistency,
distance bounds, and witness corroboration. The result is a tamper-evident
location certificate that can be used in smart contracts, supply chain
tracking, and decentralized physical infrastructure networks.

Real-life analogy
-----------------
Imagine you need to prove you visited a museum for a scavenger hunt. Instead
of taking a selfie that could be photoshopped, the museum gives you a
physical stamp on a card that uses special ink. The stamp has a unique
serial number and a time clock that is synchronized with the museum's
master clock. Other museums in the hunt can verify the stamp's authenticity
by checking the serial against the master list and confirming the time
matches their own records. You cannot forge the stamp without the special
ink, and you cannot backdate it because the clocks are synchronized. Proof
of location works the same way: specialized hardware creates hard-to-fake
evidence that other nodes can independently verify.

Tiny numeric example
--------------------
A decentralized mapping network uses radio beacons to prove location:
- Beacon A is at coordinates (10, 20) and broadcasts a signed signal every
  second.
- Beacon B is at coordinates (30, 20) and does the same.
- Alice's device is somewhere between them. It receives Beacon A's signal
  with a time-of-flight indicating 50 meters distance. It receives Beacon
  B's signal indicating 50 meters distance.
- Alice's device signs a message saying "I am at (20, 20) at timestamp
  1715000000, witnessed by Beacon A and Beacon B."
- The network checks that the distances are consistent with the claimed
  coordinate and that the beacon signatures are valid.
- If everything matches, the network mints a location NFT attesting that
  Alice's device was at (20, 20) at that time.

If Alice tried to claim she was at (100, 100), the distances to the beacons
would not match, and the proof would be rejected.

Common confusion
----------------
- Proof of location is not the same as GPS. GPS is a satellite system. Proof
  of location is a cryptographic verification layer that may use GPS as one
  input among many.
- Proof of location does not guarantee perfect accuracy. It guarantees that
  the claimed location is consistent with the evidence within a known margin
  of error.
- Proof of location hardware is not a smartphone app. It requires specialized
  radios or secure elements that resist spoofing.
- Proof of location is not just for consumers. Supply chains, insurance,
  and autonomous vehicles all need trusted location data.
- Proof of location does not reveal your location to everyone by default.
  You can prove location to a specific verifier without publishing it
  globally.
- Proof of location can use multiple mechanisms: radio time-of-flight,
  ultrasonic pulses, WiFi fingerprinting, and satellite signals.
- Proof of location is not free. It requires hardware deployment and
  network participation to maintain beacon density.

Where it appears in our code
----------------------------
`src_web3/phase50/depin_api.ts` implements an Express API that simulates
proof-of-location beacon registration, distance verification, witness
corroboration, and location attestation minting.

Key properties
--------------
- Cryptographic: Location claims are signed and tamper-evident.
- Witnessed: Multiple independent beacons corroborate the claim.
- Time-bound: Claims include precise timestamps to prevent replay attacks.
- Consistency-checked: Distance and signal measurements must align.
- Hardware-dependent: Specialized devices resist spoofing better than
  software alone.
- Privacy-preserving: Proofs can be targeted to specific verifiers.
